Case Study — 03

Empowering healthcare providers with clearer visibility into a patient's diagnostic pathway.

Over two weeks, I focused on decluttering and enhancing the user experience of the Disease Insights page, which providers found difficult to use for understanding patient outcomes.

Role UI/UX Designer
Type Client
Timeline 2 Weeks in Jun 2024
Team Will Breen, Min Sitt Thu Aung
NDA Client name withheld

Project Overview

Company Context

The company helps healthcare organizations detect diseases earlier and identify undertreated patients. I worked on "Disease Insights", a product feature that enables providers to track patient pathways leading to a diagnosis.

Outcome

I redesigned the experience with a vertical pathway layout, unified data views, and a split-screen interface, enabling users to analyze patient journeys and related insights simultaneously for faster decision-making.

Problem

The patient pathway visualization was cluttered and obscured with overlapping data tabs, forcing users to choose between viewing the full journey or critical context.

The Problem

A cluttered layout lead to a poor user experience that prevented clinicians from quickly understanding a patient's diagnostic journey.

The client's product had a feature called "Disease Insights", which aimed to allow clinicians and healthcare providers track all pathways a patient may take to reach a particular diagnosis. However, around 10 clients communicated the interface suffered from three primary problems:

Poor Visibility

Pathway visualizations were easily obscured once you open data tabs.

Inefficient Workflow

Users couldn't easily access pathway insights and contextual data at the same time.

Click-Heavy

The layout created heavy back-and-forth clicking, which slowed down a user's ability to analyze.

This set up my intentions for the re-design: provide a layout that allows the contextual data and patient pathways be viewed fully and seamlessly.

Design Direction

Changing the horizontal pathway to a vertical pathway provided an easier view from disease to diagnosis.

The horizontal pathway could only display three nodes at a time, so I opted to test a vertical layout. This layout allowed users to see more nodes at each stage and offered a more complete view of each patient's journey to diagnosis. In the examples below, all patients began their journey with an EKG test that eventually led to the disease diagnosis Mash.

I focused on confirming the design direction for the pathway with the client, and moved on to incorporating additional components of the "Disease Insights" feature once approval was received.

Iterations

Next, I focused on how to present the contextual data for each pathway.

In the original design, expanding the data panels to see more specific patient information would block the pathway entirely. With the new vertical layout, I was now focused on incorporating this component without the obstruction.

Iteration 1: Floating Data Panels

Instead of tabs that were fighting for screen real-estate, my initial idea was to create collapsable floating tabs which would still provide viewing of the pathway, but not be as obtrusive.

Iteration 2: Condensed Data Panels

For another iteration, I reorganized all data tabs into a collapsible floating panel on the left-side of the screen. To make up for the length of the panel, I added a header navigator for the user to go between different sections of the data.

Pivot

We did not move forward with these iterations as it could not support the current format of patient data.

The dataset for comorbidity data in particular included four columns: Therapeutic Area, Comorbidity, % of Patients, and Total Patients. Using my current layout iterations, the design would encounter a similar challenge as the original design — the table would appear cramped and difficult for providers to read and interpret easily.

Solution

However the client appreciated the idea of consolidating all panels to one side, which led to the concept of a split view layout in the final solution.

Split-View

I introduced a split-view design with data panels on the left and the full pathway on the right. Users can collapse either side for a full-screen view, enabling them to access relevant patient context without losing sight of the pathway and eliminates the need to click back and forth between sections.

Consolidated Data Panels Section

The data panels were also redesigned into a separate, resizable section that could accommodate an expanded data table. I retained the top navigation header and the existing collapse/expand functionality from previous iterations, enabling users to seamlessly focus on the most relevant data while hiding sections they didn't need.

Scrolling in Patient Pathway

I also mocked-up a user experience suggestion where as users scroll through nodes on the pathway, the interface also automatically snaps to the nearest node without requiring clicks. The data panel updates accordingly based on the focused node. This aims to enhance navigation by making it faster, smoother, and reducing manual interaction.

Outcome

These iterations were then submitted to the client and received positive approval!

"This is exactly what we were hoping for! The way you've handled the data panel is fantastic, and the additional enhancements truly elevate the overall user experience beyond what we have now."

— Client, Product Manager

Post-launch data is not available, but the success metrics for this redesign were centered on two measurable goals: fewer clicks required to access contextual patient data and faster diagnostic review. Both were directly targeted by the split-view layout — which eliminates the need to toggle between views — and the scroll-snap interaction, which removes manual navigation between pathway nodes.

Reflection

This project taught me that good information design isn't about what looks polished — it's about how clearly a user can think.

Two weeks goes fast, especially when a mid-project pivot means scrapping work you were confident in. But that pivot was the most useful part of the project. It forced me to stop designing around what looked organized and start designing around how a clinician actually moves through information.

What I took away most was a shift in how I think about information design. When someone is trying to understand a patient's diagnostic pathway while cross-referencing comorbidity data, the question isn't really "does this look clean?" It's "can they hold both pieces of information at once without losing track?" That became the standard I kept checking my decisions against.

I came out of this project more comfortable with constraints shaping the design rather than fighting them. The data told me what the layout needed to do, and my job was to follow that logic.
