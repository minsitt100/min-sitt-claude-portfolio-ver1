Case Study — 04

Shifting client service reps from reactive to proactive

Only 223 users had ever opened the platform's transaction feature — despite transaction issues making up 34% of all cases. We set out to find out why, and redesign the experience from the ground up.

Role Product Designer
Type Internal Product
Timeline 2025 – 2027 Launch
Company JP Morgan
Transaction Visibility — Hero Overview [Blurred for Confidentiality]

Project Overview

My Role

Product Designer embedded in a cross-functional team of researchers, engineers, and product managers. I led design from discovery through handoff — including facilitating user interviews, running usability testing sessions, and aligning with engineering on technical feasibility throughout the process.

Context

JP Morgan's internal platform is designed to be the one-stop shop for client service representatives — the people fielding calls, resolving payment issues, and maintaining relationships with the firm's most important clients. One of its features gives reps a view into their clients' transaction activity. Despite being built for exactly the kind of work reps do every day, it was almost entirely unused.

The Problem

In 2025, only 223 users had ever opened the transaction feature. Transaction-related issues account for 34% of all incoming case volume. That gap was costing reps time, clients trust, and the firm revenue.

The Outcome

We redesigned the experience around a time-based filtering model — surfacing only the transactions stuck in processing longer than expected. The solution is projected to save reps 2 hours per week, save the firm 96,000 hours firm-wide, and generate approximately $11 million in value over 3 years. It launches in 2027.

The Problem

A critical feature nobody was using — for a problem that was everywhere.

The platform's transaction feature existed to give client service reps visibility into their clients' payment activity. But in practice, it had almost no adoption. Only 223 users had ever opened it in 2025, despite transaction-related cases making up over a third of all incoming case volume.

The mismatch was striking. Transaction issues weren't rare — they were a defining part of a rep's day. So why wasn't anyone using the one tool built to help them?

A Story That Made It Real

One client. Multiple stuck wires. No way to know until they called.

During our research, one scenario kept surfacing in different forms across interviews. A client had several wires simultaneously flagged in fraud review. Due to the volume of cases on the rep's plate, there was no way to know these wires were stuck — until the client called.

What followed was a time-consuming, multi-platform scramble:

  • Searching records through a separate internal records system
  • Calling sales contacts to gather context on the client relationship
  • Looping in the fraud department and waiting for callbacks across teams
  • Manually piecing together a timeline — all while the client was waiting

By the time the issue was resolved, the client had lost confidence in the firm and stopped using one of its products entirely. The rep had done everything right. The system had failed them.

"The rep had to call three different departments just to tell the client where their money was."

This wasn't an edge case. It was a structural flaw in how reps received — and acted on — transaction information. And it was happening because there was no proactive visibility built into the platform.

Research & Discovery

Before we could design anything, we needed to understand the system — and the people inside it.

I joined this project as a new hire, which meant I had no inherited assumptions about the product, its architecture, or how reps actually worked. That turned out to be an advantage. I approached the research with genuine curiosity rather than confirmation bias.

What We Learned

Reps were operating in a fully reactive loop — and the platform was making it worse.

Across our interviews and testing sessions, two core problems emerged consistently.

Problem 1: A reactive-only workflow

Reps had no mechanism to proactively surface transaction issues. Their entire awareness of a problem depended on the client calling first. Once a call came in, they scrambled — switching between multiple platforms, making calls to several teams, and manually assembling information that should have been in one place.

Current State Flow — Rep's Reactive Workflow [Blurred for Confidentiality]

Problem 2: Too much friction to explore proactively

Even if a rep wanted to be proactive, the platform itself made it difficult. Getting from one place to another required excessive clicking. The information architecture wasn't organized around how reps thought about their work — it was organized around how the system was built internally.

Reps also had limited time in their day. They couldn't afford to go exploring on their own. Whatever the solution was, it had to surface itself — not require them to go hunting for it.

How might we shift client service reps from a reactive workflow to a proactive one — within the constraints of an already-stretched platform and engineering infrastructure?

Initial Direction

Our first solution: a unified Transaction Dashboard.

Our initial concept was a full Transaction Dashboard — a single view where reps could see all of their clients' transactions at once. Each transaction would display its current status, payment type, where it was stuck in the process, and all the essential context reps needed to communicate with clients on a given day.

The goal was to eliminate context-switching entirely. Instead of navigating across multiple internal tools, fraud teams, and sales contacts, a rep would open one view and immediately know which transactions needed attention.

It felt like the right answer. We moved into design.

The Pivot

Engineering brought us back to earth — and ultimately made the solution stronger.

When we brought the Transaction Dashboard to engineering, we hit a fundamental constraint: the platform could only load a limited number of transactions at a time. Loading all transactions for all clients simultaneously wasn't technically feasible within the current architecture.

Beyond the load issue, some of the contextual data fields we wanted to include weren't accessible through existing APIs without significant infrastructure investment — outside the project's scope and timeline.

Rather than compromise the concept into something ineffective, or ship a watered-down version that still didn't solve the core problem, we went back to the research.

Back to the Drawing Board

We returned to users with a sharper question: what actually makes a transaction worth flagging?

Instead of showing all transactions, we needed a filtering principle — one that surfaced only the transactions that required a rep's attention. If we could get that right, the load constraint became a non-issue.

We went back to our reps with targeted follow-up interviews, this time focused on a specific question: how do they distinguish a transaction that's normally in-process from one that's genuinely stuck?

Their answer was consistent across every conversation: time.

Standard processing times for different payment types were well-established and predictable to experienced reps. A wire sitting in fraud review for two hours was normal. That same wire sitting there for two days was not. A transaction progressing through formal processes at the expected pace didn't need attention. A transaction stuck in any stage longer than it should be was a clear signal.

We also went granular on the details — re-validating the exact language used for each transaction status, confirming which data fields were essential versus nice-to-have, and pressure-testing what "abnormally long" looked like for each payment type.

Engineering confirmed the approach was architecturally sound. We had our revised solution.

The Revised Solution

Time-based transaction surfacing: only show what actually needs attention.

Instead of displaying all transactions, the redesigned experience surfaces only those that have been stuck in a processing stage longer than expected for that payment type. Transactions progressing normally don't appear — which means the view reps see isn't noise. It's signal.

Every transaction that does appear comes with the essential context reps need: payment type, current stage, how long it's been there, and the information needed to give the client a clear, accurate update. No extra platform switching. No calls to other teams just to gather basic facts.

What makes this work

  • It surfaces itself. Reps don't need to go looking. The view is designed to be front-and-center, showing only what's actionable right now — so a rep can be proactive without adding to their cognitive load.
  • It respects the architecture. By filtering to anomalous transactions only, the load constraint becomes a non-issue. The platform loads a small, high-signal set of transactions rather than everything at once.
  • It was built on rep feedback. Every status label, every data field, and every time threshold was validated directly with the reps who would be using it — down to the exact phrasing of each transaction state.

Reflection

The best solution often isn't the first one.

This project reinforced something I've come to believe strongly: good design isn't about having a brilliant idea early. It's about being willing to question that idea when reality pushes back — and using that moment to ask better questions.

The engineering constraint that forced our pivot didn't weaken the solution. It sharpened it. By returning to users with a more targeted question, we found an insight — time as a proxy for abnormality — that was more elegant and more technically viable than our original direction.

It also underscored the value of investing heavily in research upfront. The 21 interviews, 15 testing sessions, and 20+ cross-functional syncs weren't just due diligence — they were the foundation that let us move quickly and confidently when we needed to change direction.
