Case Study — 02

Simplifying Financial Analysts Workflows

Designing a productivity tool that centralizes report writing and resource organization on one screen.

Role Product Design Intern
Timeline Jun 2024 – Aug 2024
Team Will Breen, Min Sitt Aung
Thumbnail Photo

Project Overview

Company Context

Curiocity aims to enhance the workflow of analysts engaging in extensive research and report writing.

Problem

Analysts across a variety of industries have an inefficient workflow characterized by constant back and forth: they have to find their sources nestled in a variety of folders and hubs, come back to their document where the report is, and repeat this process until completion.

Outcome

After interviewing 20+ users, I shipped a functional prototype that I presented to the CEO to use for funding.

User Research

The current research report writing process is frustrating and tedious for analysts.

From my initial conversations with 20 analysts in industries ranging from marketing to finance, I discovered 3 major pain points:

  1. Heavy Research Workload: Analysts manage a vast amount of reference material for report writing, with each report involving between hundreds of documents and URLs.
  2. Outdated Management Tools: Analysts typically rely on browser bookmarks and Excel files to track these resources, which proves to be time-consuming and inefficient.
  3. Distraction from Core Tasks: Labeling, sorting, and organizing research materials creates significant overhead, detracting time away from analysis.

This creates an inefficient workflow characterized by constant back-and-forth: analysts write their report, realize they need to include something they previously saw, then search for the original source.

From there, I formed my guiding "how might we" question:

How might we design a cohesive, integrated tool that can streamline the extraction and management of sources to make report writing more efficient and less burdensome?

Competitive Analysis

I first looked at what analysts currently used for their workflows to identify trends across preferred tools.

First, I sought to better understand the value propositions of existing tools for analysts to narrow down on how Curiocity could differentiate itself and stand out. There are 3 main categories these tools fall under:

Competitive Analysis — Tool Categories

From a deep dive exploration, I identified common value propositions across these categories:

  1. Content Aggregation: The ability to efficiently extract high-quality key insights from uploaded resources is a critical requirement.
  2. Report Writing: Online/offline editing and storing in-progress/completed reports on a centralized homepage.
  3. Resource Management: Flexible options to sort, filter, and tag large files are vital for effective organization and usability.

Combining these capabilities onto one platform is what Curiocity should aim to achieve!

Product Concepts

After identifying pain points and evaluating existing tools, I began mapping out product concepts and the user experience.

By this point, the CEO had already worked on some initial wireframes and we had a sync to discuss them.

Initial CEO Wireframes

Why did we not go with this version?

We decided against this version because it felt unintuitive. The report-writing section was envisioned like building website blocks, where users could add new text blocks as needed for writing paragraphs, but this created a messy layout that lacked a clean streamlined look that analysts would be familiar with.

Shortly after the discussion, I revisited the drawing board to develop a user experience journey map, strategically outlining core features and interactions which laid the foundation for the next design iteration.

User Experience Journey Map

This is also where I began thinking about how to simplify the process of report writing and file organization. I landed on a split-screen setup: the left side dedicated to writing, and the right side for managing files. This allowed users to filter and manipulate files without disrupting their writing flow, keeping everything seamlessly updated. With the core flows outlined, a new version of Curiocity was ready to be brought to life.

Homepage Design

I began designing a version of Curiocity to empower users by making their workflows more efficient and intuitive, starting with the home page.

Home Page

Introduces the split-screen concept: report-writing on the left, file organization on the right. Users can manipulate and interact with either side—for instance, creating new reports, filtering files, and editing content—without affecting the other.

Homepage — Split-Screen Design

Why this design?

This allows users to manipulate and interact with either side—for instance, creating new reports, filtering files, and editing content—without affecting the other. They can switch workflows without disruption, unlike current tools.

Report Management

I designed the report management experience to mirror the structure and flow of commonly used writing tools.

Create a Report — Step 1 of 3

(2 of 3) Create a Report

Create a Report — Step 2 of 3

(3 of 3) Create a Report

User is presented with a blank report and typical editing options to begin writing!

Create a Report — Step 3 of 3

Why this design?

I wasn't trying to reinvent the wheel—instead, I took inspiration from familiar tools like Google Docs and Microsoft Word to mirror their user experience. Since our target users were already comfortable with these platforms, I aimed to minimize onboarding friction and reduce the time it would take to get up to speed with our tool.

File Organization

Analysts work with a high volume of files, so I focused on making it easy for them to quickly sort, find, and utilize relevant and specific documents.

Search for Files

Users can search for specific keywords and see the most relevant results, with the matched terms clearly highlighted in the file names. The 'Drive' area also dynamically updates to display the corresponding files.

File Search — Keyword Highlighting

Why this design?

By adding this functionality and experience to the search, users can find the files they need faster and with less friction.

Filtering

By pressing the funnel icon, the user is presented with options on how to find files such as sorting alphabetically, files that include or exclude terms, file type, and date range.

File Filtering — Filter Options

Filtering if a File is Currently Opened

When a user opens a file, the original filter location is replaced by other files in their directory. To address this, I designed a dedicated filter modal specifically for this state.

Filtering — Dedicated Modal (File Open State)

Selecting a File

When a user selects a file, they are presented with key information such as tags, title, and a quick preview.

File Selection — Key Info Preview

Why this design?

Filtering is the primary tool users rely on to sift through their large volume of files, so I designed it to be accessible at all times—rather than limiting it to a specific view or state. Additionally, each file displays key information even after filtering, adding another layer of context to help users quickly determine relevance and refine their search as needed.

Original File vs. Summary View

When a user opens a file they are able to toggle between two views: Original File and Summary. Original File shows the full file embedded directly in Curiocity and can be manipulated. Summary View showcases condensed AI summaries of the report that the user can directly reference while they are writing.

Original File View
Summary View — AI-Condensed Notes

Why this design?

This design was created to enhance productivity by reducing the need to switch between multiple windows or tabs. By allowing users to simultaneously view the full document and a concise summary, it helps them focus on writing while easily referencing essential information—streamlining the workflow and minimizing cognitive load.

Learnings

There were a couple of things I would do differently with more time.

User Research to Iterate on Designs

Due to the short time frame and the nature of my involvement, the goal was to quickly build an MVP rather than conduct extended user research or multiple iterations. Since I wasn't going to remain on the team long term, the client preferred that I focus on delivering a functional proof of concept that their team could build upon after my departure.

Collaborating with Engineering

While I didn't have the opportunity to work with engineers for this project, a process I would like to add would be to showcase my designs to them early on to ensure I am blending creativity with feasibility and functionality.

Reflection

Curiocity was the first project where I saw a product vision take shape through my design.

Starting with a blank canvas gave me the creative freedom to shape the design from the ground up. It also pushed me to further develop my skills in Figma, especially with advanced features like animations, such as creating a loading screen! This project not only strengthened my design skills but also honed my strategic thinking to come up with features for the product to achieve its goal. It was an exciting and incredibly rewarding learning experience, and I look forward to applying these lessons to future projects!
